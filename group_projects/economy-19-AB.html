[Note: Paragraphs 19a within the chapter "Economy" in Walden highlight revisions that Thoreau made across Versions A, B, and the final published version. Throughout these revisions Henry David Thoreau speaks with passion about the subject of luxury and material comfort, making his philosophical views against these forms of luxury known. He argues that simplicity allows for wisdom, independence, and moral growth. Thoreau used deliberate efforts to transform his emotional personal critique of luxury into a more polished philosophical argument, which presents a simplistic ideology, rather than a universally known moral idea. To ensure an improvement in the composition process, when making these changes among versions, Thoreau consistently clarifies awkward syntax, removes excessive emotional rhetoric, stregthens philosophical precision, broadens the universality of his ideas, and emphasized lived experiences over intellectual performance. Within Walden, philosophy becomes far more than an abstract thought for Thoreau. He believes and wants to spread the message to others, that true philosophy is a way of living, and requires being grounded in simplicity, having self-reliance, and taking deliberate actions that honor ways of simplicity. ]

To the elevation and ennoblement

of man kind what are called the luxuries &

many of the comforts of life are not only

indispensable, but positive hindrances.

In paragraph 19a of the first version of Walden, version A, Thoreau sets the stage for his ideology and philosophy behind the negative effects that luxuries and comforts have on people. He believes that luxury and comfort are "hindrances", and that we must take pleasure in the simple things in life, and not get caught up in materialistic goods and the want to always have more. Throughout all of Walden, Thoreau sends readers the message that less is more in life.

In version B of Walden, Thoreau makes changes, deletes, and edits many sentences from version A of paragraph 19a. In the start of paragraph 19a, Thoreau focuses mostly on the harmful effects of luxury and comfort, using lots of broad statements. Later in the paragraph, however, and in further paragraphs of "Economy" he goes on to define what a true philosopher is.

Respect to luxuries and comforts, the

and meager life than the poor. The

ancient philosophers, Chinese, Hindoo, Persian and Greek were a class of

men than which none have been poorer

In paragraph 19a of the first version of Walden, version A, Thoreau makes broad statements that are composed in awkward and complicated ways. In the first version of paragraph 19a, Thoreau writes "To the elevation and ennoblement of mankind what are called the luxuries & many of the comforts of life are not only indispensable, but positive hindrances." In the second version, or version B, he goes on to revise this sentence to be "Most of the luxuries, and many of the so-called comforts of life, are not only not indispensable, but positive hindrances to the elevation of mankind." In making this change, along with others, Thoreau changes the focus of the sentence, and makes his argument clearer. By introducing luxuries and comforts early on in the sentence within version B, Thoreau's argument becomes more direct. When Thoreau originally wrote about ancient philosophers, he spoke very broadly, but in version B he adds in that the philsophers were "Chinese, Hindoo, Persian, and Greek", making a more detailed claim and section to the paragraph.
