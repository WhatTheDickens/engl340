The revisions made between version A of Spring 11 and its published counterpart establishes the idea that spring's emergence is focused, cyclical, and interconnected. Consequently, it highlights Thoreau's perspective and how he uses the season of spring to model how society functions like an ecosystem whose progress depends on recognizing natural patterns and choosing renewal, action, and cooperation.

[Note: In pencil, Thoreau has written "v end of sand foliage At length the sun's rays"]

But we must not let

the winter go so early. When

the ground is completely partically bare of

snow and a few warm days have

dried it's surface here and there it is pleasant

to compare the faint first tender signs

of the infant year just peeping

forth, with the stately beauty of

the withered vegetation which has

withstood the winter-

Thoreau's revisions make Spring feel more decisive and direct. Instead of a hesitant, cluttered transition, spring becomes a clear moment of renewal. This supports the reoccuring theme that spring models the kind of purposeful renewal humans must choose in society. Nature has a tendancy to reach a level of equilibrium and balance through its own processes. By removing the chaotic, tangled ecosystem catalog and replacing it with a more balanced alternative, the revisions Thoreau makes serve to further demonstrate his perspective on the human experience as a eventual harmonious ecosystem.

life ever lasting asters, goldenrods, and graceful wild grasses whose winter is more stately than their summer even, as if not till then their beauty was ripe The various

thistles sedges and other strong stemmed

plants, which have not even yet

sown their seeds- and graceful

reeds and rushes whose cotton grass cattail mulleins hardhack meadow-sweet. winter is

more gay and stately than their [Note: passage continues onto manuscript page 234 ]

summer - as if not till then was

their beauty ripe. - Wild oats per

chance and life - everlasting whose

autumn has jsut arrived - Those un

exhausted granaries of winter, whose

seeds entertain the earliest birds.

In this section of Walden, Thoreau's revision shifts the tone from chaotic abundance to a coherent interdependence. This demonstrates the unique perspective of how society functions like an ecosystem, but also one whose patterns can be recognized and understood, not random or overwhelming. The version A manuscript places a lot of emphasis on the plants being "more stately" by repeating it throughout the passage. This is eventually cut down to being used only once in the published edition. Thoreau does not want the reader to see winter as a sign of death, but as a necessary stage in a cycle. This further supports the idea of stagnation, both in nature and a societal context, are a necessary step that leads to renewal and progress.
