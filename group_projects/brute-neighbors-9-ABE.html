"Brute Neighbors" appears as early as Draft A of Walden, where it begins following "Spring" 26b. By Draft E this section has become paragraph 9, and Thoreau has added an additional 8 paragraphs before it. In the exerpts below we see Thoreau both cross out and add via pencil large sections of text. These changes demonstrate Thoreau adding additional reflections on the "neighbors" of Walden pond, as well as removing reflections for overall text coherence.

At the top this page from A, Thoreau has penciled part of paragraph 9 between the lines of "Spring" 26b. Implication: after penning the rest of paragraph 9, Thoreau returned to add this encounter with the bird's nest. This may have been done to ease the transition between "Spring", which discussed birds, and the closer animal encounters of "Brute Neighbors" 9.

Thoreau describes two experiences that reveal different aspects of nature. The story of the bird nest suggests a struggle between human desire to help nature and our own lack of understanding of it. The story of the mouse demonstrates the intimate and trusting relationship humans can have with nature, as well as the way many things in nature are similar and connected (comparing the mouse to both a fly and a squirrel).

This segment is again carried over from version A. It appears that after penning this section, Thoreau decided to add more information about the kind of mice he was discussing. To avoid redundancy he replaced "a long-eared red-bellied field mouse" with "one of these", likely after adding the pencil text. Perhaps the addition of referencing the peculiarity of the mouse, as well as the interest of a naturalist friend, was intended to highlight how special the nature of Walden pond was, and how it differed from Thoreau's previous natural experiences.
